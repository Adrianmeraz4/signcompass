Lesson 1: Introduction

What is ASL?

ASL or American Sign Language is a non-verbal language used by the deaf and mute community in the US. ASL uses a combination of hand signs,
directional motions, and gestures to convey language and meaning. Around 430,000 people In the US use ASL to communicate and now you can
too. This introductory lesson will teach you basic information about ASL before learning any signs, the information is crucial still so
pay attention.

Static vs Dynamic

In American Sign Language there are two types of signs: Static and Dynamic

Static signs rely on the use of the shape and direction of your hands and fingers and are typically reserved for letters, numbers, and simple
words. This Introductory will primarily focus on these signs.

Dynamic signs incorporate gestures and movements with shape and direction to symbolize larger words or even strings of them.

Importance of Orientation

Verbal language uses several letters to compose endless combinations. The way sign languages approach this is similar, using the hand direction
and fingers to represent the particular word. Just like the words brood and broad are only a one letter difference, signs do the same, the
difference between the letter A and S is the position of the thumb in the sign.

Hand Direction

The direction of the hand is of great importance, not only because it can change the word entirely, it also is crucial in the readability of your
signs. Think of a limp wrist symbolizing a slurred word, it can be harder to read and understand if your sign isn't properly displayed. When
displaying a sign, your hand should be steady with your palm typically facing your listener and each sign should be completed and pronounced before
starting the next

Finger identification

Throughout these lessons you will have instructions on how to arrange your fingers to produce the sign being taught. The Fingers will be labeled
from Thumb to pinkie and your right hand to produce the signs. The Fingers number assignment will be shown below
